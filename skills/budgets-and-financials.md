# Budgets & Financials

A budget is the project plan expressed in numbers. It doesn't have to be perfect — it has to show what
the project will do and what it will cost to do it *well, safely, and sustainably*. Clarity here builds
trust: if a cost can't be explained in one plain sentence, the ask is too vague.

The most common early-stage mistake is **under-budgeting** — leaving out coordination time, operations,
and safety — which leads to filling the gap personally and burning out. A smaller number doesn't
necessarily make a grant easier to win; a credible, well-justified number does.

## How to build one

Start from the work plan: list what you'll do over the next 6–12 months, then attach a cost to each
step. For each line, be able to say in one sentence what it's for and why it matters.

**Get real prices.** Use receipts, local supplier quotes, online listings, transport fares, or two
quick quotes for anything expensive or unfamiliar (a screenshot of a vendor's price counts). Past
spending on similar activities is often the best predictor.

**Add contingency.** Travel costs change, things break, timelines slip. Include a 5–10% line named
"Contingency" or "Unforeseen costs."

## The four cost types people forget to name

1. **Project costs** — visible, on-the-ground delivery: materials, tools, transport, venue hire,
   data/airtime, printing, small equipment.
2. **Operational costs** — what makes the work possible and sustainable: coordination time, basic
   admin, communications, internet, finance support, safeguarding, translation, design. These are not
   "extras."
3. **Growth costs** — what strengthens the project over time: training, reusable equipment, piloting
   something new, improving systems, careful expansion to a second site.
4. **Safety & wellbeing costs** — real project costs. Every project carries some risk (online or
   offline): safer travel, secure communications, data protection, safeguarding, legal advice,
   insurance, protective equipment, peer support.
   - If a safety cost is needed to *run an activity* (e.g. safer travel to a site, protective gear),
     put it under **project costs**.
   - If it's behind the scenes supporting the whole project (insurance, safeguarding, secure comms,
     legal advice), put it under **operational costs**.

## Pay people for their time

Early-stage leaders often leave their own labour out of the budget, then struggle to keep going.
Coordinating, reporting, organising, and consistent fieldwork are real labour. Depending on the funder,
call it a **stipend, part-time coordination, or project management support** — the point stands: the
project needs people's time to run.

Volunteers are valuable, but relying on unpaid labour limits who can take part. Where possible, budget
stipends, food/transport, or time subsidies so participation isn't restricted to those who can afford
to work for free.

## Name in-kind support

If something is donated — venue, volunteer time, materials — name it as **"in-kind support."** It shows
community backing and helps funders understand the project's true cost. But don't design a plan that
only works if everyone works for free.

## Sizing the ask

Three checks:
1. **Be honest about real cost** — reflect what it takes to do the work properly and safely.
2. **Always check the funder's requirements** — caps, fixed amounts, allowable/disallowed costs,
   co-funding or match requirements. (Rigidity here is also a signal of how the funder operates.)
3. **Make the budget match the plan** — every activity in the narrative should have a cost, and every
   cost should map to an activity.

Two ways to shape the ask when unsure:
- **Levels of support:** a minimum (essentials to do the work properly) and a growth option (what would
  make it more stable or extend reach).
- **Cost one clear strand:** if you run several activities, fully cost one programme (e.g. education
  sessions or restoration days) and ask for that. Specific asks are easier to say yes to.

## A simple budget table

Adapt columns to the funder's template. A clean default:

| Category | Item | Quantity | Unit cost | Total | Notes (what it's for / why) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Project | Mangrove seedlings | 500 | 0.50 | 250.00 | Stock for 3 replanting sites |
| Project | Transport to sites | 12 trips | 15.00 | 180.00 | Team + volunteers to coastal sites |
| Operational | Coordination (part-time) | 6 months | 200.00 | 1,200.00 | Lead organiser time |
| Operational | Comms & data | 6 months | 20.00 | 120.00 | Reporting, updates, monitoring |
| Safety & wellbeing | Insurance | 1 | 150.00 | 150.00 | Field activity cover |
| Growth | Volunteer training | 1 workshop | 300.00 | 300.00 | Monitoring skills, sustainability |
| Contingency | Unforeseen costs (~8%) | — | — | 176.00 | Price changes, breakage |
| **Total** | | | | **2,376.00** | |

If the funder wants a spreadsheet deliverable, build it with the `xlsx` skill; if they accept a table,
the above is enough. Include an **income/co-funding** section if relevant (other grants, donations,
seed funding, in-kind) so the funder sees the full picture.

## Compliance details that catch people out

When a grant is offered, check (and where reasonable, negotiate — it's normal to ask):
- **Pre-financing vs. reimbursement** — some grants reimburse after you spend. If you can't front
  costs, ask for an upfront instalment or direct vendor payment.
- **Proof required** — receipts, invoices, photos, attendance lists, and how organised they must be.
- **Audit** — if required, check who pays and whether it belongs in the budget.
- **Budget flexibility** — permission to move a small % between lines without approval.
- **Payment schedule** — a larger first instalment, or payments timed to when costs occur.
- **Proportionate reporting** — matched to the grant size.

## Match funding (in brief)

Match funding is when a funder agrees to match what you raise (often doubling it, up to a cap). It makes
each gift feel larger and signals existing trust. Make it concrete and time-bound: state the target, the
window, and what happens when you hit it; tie it to a clear moment (launch, milestone, season). A simple
message ("Your donation is doubled today") usually beats a long explanation. See the match-funding ask
template in `templates.md`.

## Building a resilient funding mix

Projects rarely thrive on one source. A practical mix:
- **One pot for core costs** — unrestricted funding, recurring donors, or a reliable partner.
- **Project grants** — for clearly costed activities (workshops, restoration days, equipment).
- **A gap-filler** — earned income and in-kind support (space, materials, food/transport, volunteer
  time) to keep things moving between grants.

Even small earned income strengthens an application — it shows multiple streams of support and reduces
dependency on any single funder.

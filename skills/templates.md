# Templates

Copy-paste starting points. Replace every `[bracket]` with the applicant's real, confirmed details —
never leave invented figures. Keep the wording in the applicant's voice; these are scaffolds, not
finished text. Save the strongest versions in one place so they can be reused and tailored across
applications (it keeps the story consistent and saves time).

## 1. One-line mission

> *[What you do] + for [who it's for] + to [the change you're aiming for].*

Reusable everywhere. It's also the seed of your Theory of Change.

## 2. 4-sentence project description

> **[Project name]** supports **[community/group]** by **[what you do]**.
> So far, we have **[X people reached / key impact stat]**.
> We use **[your approach/method]** because **[why it matters / why it fits your community]**.
> Our goal is **[the big, long-term impact you're working toward]**.

## 3. Theory of Change builder

Fill the blanks, then connect them:

- **Impact (long-term):** In ~5 years, we want to see: `[___]`
- **Outcomes (6–24 months):** What will be different for people/nature/community: `[___]`
- **Activities (now):** What we actually do regularly: `[___]`

> **One-sentence ToC:** Because we **[activities]**, we expect to see **[outcomes]**, which contributes
> to **[impact]**.

Worked example: *"Because we run restoration activities and train volunteers, we expect to see healthier
mangrove coverage and increased community engagement, contributing to long-term ecosystem recovery."*

| Safety & wellbeing | `[insurance / secure comms / etc.]` | `[qty]` | `[cost]` | `[total]` | `[reason]` |
| Growth | `[training / equipment]` | `[qty]` | `[cost]` | `[total]` | `[reason]` |
| Contingency | Unforeseen costs (5–10%) | — | — | `[total]` | Price changes, breakage, delays |

Add an **Income / co-funding** block (other grants, donations, seed funding, in-kind) if showing the
full funding picture. See `budgets-and-financials.md` for what to include and how to size the ask.

## 5. Cover letter / email to a funder

> Subject: `[Project name]` — `[one-line what + the ask, e.g. "youth-led mangrove restoration, $5,000"]`
>
> Dear `[correct name / team]`,
>
> `[1 sentence: who you are and what you do — the one-line mission.]`
> `[2–3 sentences: the problem and your approach, with one real proof point.]`
> `[1 sentence — the alignment link: how your work advances THEIR stated priority.]`
> `[1 sentence — what their funding would unlock for the project, tied to the ask amount.]`
> `[1 sentence: a clear, simple next step — e.g. "Our full proposal is attached / I'd welcome a short
> call to see if this is a fit."]`
>
> With thanks,
> `[Name, role, project, contact, link]`

Keep it short, specific, and warm. Get the funder's name right.

## 6. Concept note (1–2 pages)

A short pre-proposal many funders request first. Sections:

1. **Summary** — Problem → Approach → Proof → Ask, in 3–5 sentences.
2. **The problem / need** — specific, local, quantified where honest.
3. **What we'll do** — main activities and why this approach fits.
4. **Who we are** — team, lived experience, track record, registration/host status.
5. **Expected change** — one-sentence Theory of Change + 2–3 outcomes.
6. **The ask & budget snapshot** — amount, what it funds, timeframe.
7. **Alignment** — one sentence on fit with the funder's priorities.

## 7. Elevator pitch (Nat Geo structure)

A 30–90 second spoken pitch. Four beats:

1. **Self-reflection & know your audience** — include your *why*. Who are they? Are they a decision-maker?
   What do they already know? What's the setting? What are you hoping for from them?
2. **Make them care** — the problem and why it's urgent; show you know the topic; how your project makes
   an impact.
3. **Call to action** — what you want them to take away or do from this specific interaction.
4. **Practice** — rehearse out loud. It's not just what you say, but how you say it.

Fill-in opener:
> "**[Project name]** supports **[community/group]** by **[what you do]**. So far, we've **[impact stat]**.
> We use **[approach]** because **[why]**. Our goal is **[change]** — and **[the specific ask / next step]**."

## 8. Pitch-deck outline

Keep slides simple and uncluttered; balance facts (impact, reach, growth) with emotion (human stories,
quotes, lived experience). Use real project photos — authenticity beats polish.

1. **Why we exist** — the problem; why it matters to you and your community.
2. **Our impact** — your best photos + 1–2 bold impact numbers, with a quote or short story.
3. **Our growth** — new partners, more participants, expanded reach, stronger systems (an infographic helps).
4. **Our collaboration** — how your work fits *their* funding priorities; why they're a great partner. Make
   the connection explicit.
5. **Our ask** — the amount (or range) linked to your budget, and what it unlocks. For an early intro call,
   focus on fit first and follow up with the ask.
6. **An invitation** — a simple closing slide inviting partnership; thanks; next steps; links.

## 9. Match-funding ask

> "Our project, **[project name]**, supports **[community/group]** by **[what you do]**. So far, we have
> **[X reached / key stat]**. We recently received a grant of **[amount]** to support **[purpose]**. If we
> can double this, we'll be able to **[expand impact: reach more people, add workshops, increase resources]**.
> Would you be interested in helping us grow our impact by matching this grant?"

Make it concrete and time-bound: state the target, the window, and what happens when you hit it.

## 10. Story checklist

Before submitting any story-driven section, confirm:
- [ ] I explain why I care about this issue, in a real, human way.
- [ ] I can describe the project in 2–3 clear sentences.
- [ ] I include one example or one impact number (real and confirmed).
- [ ] I have one main message I return to again and again.
- [ ] The story is clear, authentic, and consistent across formats.
- [ ] I have consent for any other person's story; no risky disclosure of identities/locations.

## 11. Interview / donor-meeting prep

Treat a donor meeting as a two-way fit conversation. Prepare:
- **Three key messages** you want them to remember.
- **Three examples/stories** you can use (one challenge faced, one win you're proud of, one lesson learned).
- **Three questions** to ask them, e.g.:
  - What inspires you to fund projects like ours?
  - How do you like to work with grantees beyond funding?
  - Would you be open to quick feedback on a short concept note?
  - Are there other funders/programmes you'd suggest — and would you introduce us?

Logistics: bring 2–3 people max with assigned roles (story/impact, plan, budget); request an interpreter a
week ahead if needed; test connectivity and background for online calls; be ready to name an amount (or
range) tied to your budget.

## 12. Reporting & change requests

**Lightweight report skeleton** (most reports ask the same four things):
- *What we did* (1–2 lines) · *What changed* (one number or real example/quote/photo) · *What we learned /
  will do next* (1 line). Keep it proportionate to the grant size.

**Plans changed? Ask for an amendment early** (funders prefer adjusting to watching you struggle):
> "Plans have shifted slightly due to **[reason]**. We're still aiming for **[same goal]**. Could we agree
> an amendment to **[timeline/budget]** so we can deliver well? We propose **[new dates / revised lines]**."

- *No-cost extension* = more time, same budget. *Cost-plus extension* = more time + more funding.

**Renewal ask** (raise it before the final report — ideally 6–8 weeks before the grant ends):
> "We're starting to plan the next phase. Would you be open to discussing renewed support, or is there
> someone else you think we should speak to?"

# Storytelling & Persuasion

Funders rarely know the applicant's country, context, or daily reality. Storytelling is how you
translate the work so a stranger can *understand the problem, picture the change, and feel why it
matters*. A strong story is **clear** (understood quickly), **authentic** (sounds like the person),
and **consistent** (doesn't change across forms and platforms).

Use these frameworks to shape and pressure-test a story. They complement — never replace — the
applicant's real voice and real facts.

## 1. The "why" layers

When shaping a story, work through four layers. You don't need all four in every answer, but having
them ready makes any section stronger.

- **The spark** — What did you see or experience that made you want to act? What problem are you
  solving? (This is the emotional root; lead with it when the funder values lived experience.)
- **Why you** — What skills, knowledge, or lived experience make you well placed to lead this? Small
  wins and real-world learning often matter more than a polished brand.
- **The reality** — Where you work, who's involved, what a normal day looks like. Just enough detail
  to make it real and credible.
- **One proof point** — One or two solid numbers, a short example of change, or a community quote.

Passion plus evidence beats either alone. Many first-time applicants over-index on "correct" language
and proving impact, and forget that authentic, personal conviction is what makes people *care* — and
caring is what gets read closely and funded.

## 2. The narrative arc (Problem → Approach → Proof → Ask)

The backbone of nearly every proposal, cover letter, concept note, and pitch:

- **Problem** — what's happening and why it matters. Specific and human, not abstract.
- **Approach** — what you do, and why it fits this community/context.
- **Proof of change** — one proof point: a number, a real example, a quote, or a short impact story.
- **Ask** — what you need and what it unlocks, linked to the budget.

Keep the *heart* of this constant across funders; change only the emphasis and the language to match
who's reading.

## 3. ABT: And, But, Therefore

A compact way to give any narrative tension and momentum. Fill in:

> *[Context] **AND** [more context], **BUT** [the problem/tension], **THEREFORE** [your approach and
> what changes].*

Example: *"Our coastal community depends on mangroves for fishing and storm protection, **and** these
forests have sheltered families for generations, **but** a third have been cleared in a decade,
**therefore** we run community-led replanting with local fishers to restore them."*

If a story has no "but," it has no stakes; if it has no "therefore," it has no point. ABT is a fast
diagnostic.

## 4. Made to Stick (SUCCESs)

Heath & Heath's checklist for ideas that stick. Use it to sharpen a key message:

- **Simple** — one core idea, not five. What's the single thing they should remember?
- **Unexpected** — a surprising fact or angle that breaks the reader's autopilot.
- **Concrete** — sensory, specific detail (500 seedlings, 80% survival) over abstraction (resilience).
- **Credible** — real numbers, named partners, a verifiable example, a community voice.
- **Emotional** — make them care about *people and place*, not statistics alone.
- **Stories** — a short, true narrative beats a list of attributes.

## 5. Before–After–Bridge & STAR impact stories

Two reliable shapes for a short impact story (use real events only):

- **Before–After–Bridge:** the situation before → the situation after your work → the "bridge" (what
  you did that made the difference).
- **STAR:** **S**ituation, **T**ask, **A**ction, **R**esult. Good for "describe a time you…" prompts
  and for capacity/track-record sections.

One concrete, true micro-story (a turning point, a small win, a person whose situation changed) is
worth more than several adjectives.

## 6. The explicit alignment link (the persuasion hinge)

The single most important persuasive move in grant writing: **make the fit explicit.**

> Sentence 1: *How your work advances the funder's stated priority.*
> Sentence 2: *What their funding would unlock for your project.*

This reframes the relationship from "please give me money" to "here is how we help each other meet our
goals." Reviewers are scoring exactly this. Build the link from the funder's *own stated priorities*
(see the funder-fit reference), but phrase it in the applicant's voice — never parrot their slogans.

## 7. Cialdini's principles — used ethically

Robert Cialdini's principles of influence, applied to funding *honestly* (every claim must be true):

- **Social proof** — name credible partners, prior funders, or peer organizations that back the work.
- **Authority** — show relevant expertise, lived experience, or endorsements that establish standing.
- **Liking** — warmth and shared values; genuine connection to the funder's mission.
- **Reciprocity** — offer real mutual value (visibility, learning, community access, co-created results).
- **Commitment/consistency** — show a track record of following through on what you said you'd do.
- **Scarcity/urgency** — a *real* time window, season, or milestone (a launch, a match deadline, an
  ecological window) explains why "now." Never manufacture false urgency.
- **Unity** — shared identity or belonging ("we're part of this community/movement").

Use these to decide *what true things to surface and where*, not to spin. Manufactured urgency,
borrowed credibility, or inflated social proof backfires the moment a reviewer checks.

## 8. Choosing proof points

You don't need a lot of data. Pick proof that is **real, specific, and verifiable**:
- A number that captures scale or change (people reached, area restored, survival rate, repeat
  participation, funds matched).
- A short example of one thing that changed for one person/place.
- A direct community quote (with consent).

If data is thin, that's fine — show *what changed, why it matters, and who it reached*. Where the work
is early-stage, traction signals (partnerships, pilots, demand, prior small wins) are legitimate proof.

## 9. Common voice/story mistakes

- **Flattening the voice** with generic AI phrasing — reviewers notice, and it reads as inauthentic.
- **Overgeneralised goals** — "raise awareness," "support communities." Always add who, where, how
  many, by when, what will be different.
- **Burying the lede** — leading with history/process instead of problem and change.
- **All emotion or all data** — strong stories balance facts (impact, reach, growth) with feeling
  (human stories, quotes, lived experience).
- **Telling other people's stories without consent** — and exposing identities or locations that
  create risk. Anonymise, aggregate, or verify privately when needed.

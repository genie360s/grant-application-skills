# Proposal Anatomy

Most funding applications — whether a short concept note or a long institutional proposal — ask for
the same underlying things in different orders and depths: *What's the problem? What will you do? What
will change? How will you know? Can you deliver? What will it cost?*

Use the funder's own section names and order when they give them. When they don't, this is a reliable
default structure. Always write each section to its **question and word limit**, and make sure the
**evaluation criteria** are each visibly addressed somewhere.

## 1. Summary / abstract

Write it last; place it first. In 3–6 sentences: the problem, your approach, the change you expect,
who you are, and the ask. It should stand alone — many reviewers decide their initial lean here.
Use the **Problem → Approach → Proof → Ask** arc.

## 2. Need / problem statement

Establish that a real, specific problem exists and that this applicant understands it intimately.
- Lead with the concrete reality, not abstractions. Quantify the problem where you honestly can.
- Show *proximity*: lived experience, community relationships, or local data that establish standing.
- Connect to the funder's priority area without forcing it.
- Avoid "raise awareness"-type framings; name who is affected, where, and how.

## 3. Goals & SMART objectives

A **goal** is the big-picture change. **Objectives** are the specific, measurable steps toward it.
Make objectives **SMART**: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.

- Weak: "Improve the local environment."
- SMART: "Establish 500 mangrove seedlings across 3 sites with ≥80% survival by June 2026, and train
  20 youth volunteers in monitoring."

Keep objectives realistic and visibly tied to the activities and budget. Over-promising is a credibility
risk; reviewers reward plans that look deliverable.

## 4. Approach / activities / methods

What you will actually do, in sequence, and why this approach fits the context.
- List the main activities; for each, you should be able to say in one sentence what it is and why it
  matters.
- Show *why this approach* (community-led, locally rooted, evidence-based) rather than a generic plan.
- Make it believable: who does what, where, how many, by when. Specificity signals deliverability.
- Where relevant, note what you'll do differently from approaches that have failed before.

## 5. Theory of Change & logic model

A **Theory of Change (ToC)** explains the change you want and how your work gets there. It doesn't have
to be a diagram — a few clear sentences work.

Build it in layers:
- **Impact** (long-term): the difference you're working toward (e.g. "local mangrove ecosystems are
  restored and protected").
- **Outcomes** (6–24 months): visible changes you can point to (more volunteers, reduced waste, fishers
  adopting new methods).
- **Activities** (now): what you regularly do (planting days, training, working with fishers).

One-sentence ToC:
> *"Because we [activities], we expect to see [outcomes], which contributes to [impact]."*

The classic **logic model** chain is: **Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact.** Institutional
funders may also ask for a **logframe** (logical framework) with indicators, means of verification, and
assumptions for each level — read their template and mirror it exactly.

## 6. Monitoring, Evaluation & Learning (MEL)

Show you'll know whether it's working — primarily for the applicant and community, not just the funder.

- **Monitoring** = what you did (activities, participation, outputs).
- **Evaluation** = what changed (results, what worked/didn't).
- **Learning** = what you'll do next (keep, change, stop).

A simple approach that works for most early-stage projects: choose **2–5 indicators** (a few numbers
plus one or two stories); after each activity or monthly, save one proof point (a number, quote, photo,
or short example); write one line of learning ("next time we will…").

Examples of meaningful indicators by domain:
- *Restoration/biodiversity:* area restored, seedlings planted + survival rate, species sightings,
  water-quality results, patrol days.
- *Waste/pollution:* kg collected, sites cleaned, households reached, waste diverted, repeat participation.
- *Education/community:* sessions delivered, attendance + repeat attendance, feedback, behaviour-change examples.
- *Advocacy/systems:* meetings held, commitments secured, policy steps, new partners mobilised.

Make reporting proportionate to the grant size — and propose formats that fit real life (a short update,
photos, a voice note + transcript) where the funder allows.

## 7. Sustainability & scalability

Funders want to know the value outlasts the grant. Address, honestly:
- How the work continues after this funding (earned income, in-kind support, recurring donors, a
  diversified funding mix, community ownership, embedded systems).
- What could scale or replicate, and what would be needed — without over-claiming.
- That the project addresses a real community need, not just a funding opportunity.

## 8. Organizational capacity / team / track record

Make the case that *this team* can deliver. Funders back the people as much as the project.
- Relevant skills, lived experience, and community trust.
- Track record: prior small wins, pilots, partnerships, past grants delivered.
- Registration status and, if unregistered, the path (fiscal host/sponsor) — see budgets/fit references.
- Use STAR or before–after–bridge for a short, true delivery story.
- If the funder offers capacity support (mentoring, training) and the applicant wants it, say so.

## 9. Budget & justification

Covered in depth in `budgets-and-financials.md`. Key principle for the narrative: every cost should be
explainable in one plain sentence, the total should match the plan, and operational/coordination costs
should be visible — not hidden or omitted.

## 10. Risks, safeguarding & ethics

Many funders ask, and strong applicants address it unprompted where relevant:
- **Risks** to delivery and how you'll mitigate them.
- **Safeguarding** of children, young people, and vulnerable people; consent and dignity in storytelling.
- **Data/security** for the team and community, especially in sensitive contexts (land conflict, advocacy).
- Where public disclosure creates risk, state that you'll verify privately (references, partner contacts,
  a call) rather than publishing identities or locations.

## 11. Tailoring depth to the opportunity type

- **Microgrants / seed funding / prizes:** short. Lead with the story, one proof point, a clear specific
  ask, and a simple budget. Don't over-write.
- **Fellowships / scholarships:** weight the personal "why," trajectory, and what the support unlocks for
  the individual; tie it to the programme's mission.
- **Project grants (restricted):** full anatomy; tight objectives–activities–budget alignment; clear MEL.
- **Core / unrestricted / trust-based:** emphasise mission, capacity, and the value of flexibility; lighter
  on line-item justification, heavier on strategy and track record. (You can ask about flexibility even
  when a call looks fixed.)
- **Institutional / public funds (UN, government, multilaterals):** expect logframes, strict eligibility,
  accredited-partner routes, and heavy compliance — mirror their templates precisely and consider applying
  via an intermediary if early-stage.
- **Accelerators / pitch competitions:** emphasise traction, team, model, and scalability; prepare a deck
  and verbal pitch (see templates).

# Funder Fit & Criteria Check

Two disciplines decide most outcomes before a word of prose matters: **applying to the right funders**,
and **writing to (then checking against) the actual criteria**. This file covers how to research a
funder, how to read the relationship honestly (green/red flags), and how to map a draft to the rubric
before submitting.

## Part A — Research the funder before investing effort

Spend time understanding who the funder is and what they actually support *before* drafting. This saves
energy and lets you tailor so the pitch lands. If a funder name or URL is given, **web-search to confirm
current priorities and recent grants** — funder strategies change.

Pull out three things, minimum:
1. **What they fund** — themes, target population, funding type (restricted vs. core, project vs. seed,
   individual vs. organisation), and the language they use to describe the work they back.
2. **Where they fund** — geography and any regional limits.
3. **What they choose** — look at **recent grantees**: size (grassroots vs. large NGO), stage, geography,
   and whether they fund early-stage work. This is the fastest way to tell if the applicant is in their
   world.

Then decide what kind of support actually helps: a one-off push for a moment, or longer-term support? A
light-touch funder, or a more involved partner?

**Using AI/search for funder research (verify everything on the funder's own site):**
- "Summarise what this funder supports, where they fund, and any eligibility rules to watch for."
- "Based on their past grants, what kinds of projects do they prefer, and what do they avoid?"
- "Based on their mission and strategy, what parts of my project are a strong match for this funder?"

**Eligibility & access notes:**
- If not registered as a nonprofit, only apply to opportunities that fund individuals or explicitly
  accept unregistered projects — or use a **fiscal host/sponsor** (a registered org that receives the
  grant on your behalf, often adding credibility and handling some admin, usually for a fee). Start
  fiscal-host conversations early; they take time.
- Map the **funding ceiling** in the applicant's context. Larger grants often require registration;
  staying informal (or using a host) can protect time and flexibility below that ceiling.
- Legitimate grants **don't charge application fees.** Flag any that do.

## Part B — Reading the funder relationship (reverse due diligence)

Funders do **due diligence** on applicants (checking who you are, how you'll use funds, risks). You can
do the same in return — checking values, how they treat grantees, and what reporting looks like.

**Green flags (good signs):**
- **Values align naturally** — you don't need to reshape your work to fit their agenda.
- **Trust-based partnership** — they listen, act on feedback, treat change as learning.
- **Real duty of care** — clear safeguarding/whistleblowing routes; they'll fund safety & wellbeing costs.
- **Fair, proportionate reporting** — matched to grant size; flexible formats (video, photos, voice note
  + transcript).

**Red flags (caution):**
- **Forcing a fit** — pushing you to change goals to match their call.
- **Controlling/micromanaging** — questioning every decision or restricting spending in ways that block
  delivery.
- **Lack of protection** — no safeguarding, whistleblowing, or duty-of-care policy.
- **Admin-heavy for no reason** — long proposals for small grants, endless check-ins, reporting that
  doesn't match the funding.
- **Application fees** — a hard stop.

If you spot red flags, name them to the user. Sometimes the right move is to apply anyway with eyes open;
sometimes it's to walk away and protect the applicant's time.

## Part C — The fit check (do this before drafting)

Write these clearly. If you can't, fit is weak or the pitch needs sharpening:
- One sentence linking the project to *this funder's stated priorities* (in the applicant's words).
- One sentence on what the money would do for the project.
- Confirm: Do they fund work like this, in this place, at this stage? Does the applicant meet eligibility?
  Is the amount worth the time to apply and report?

Strategy: **find ~10 opportunities, then shortlist ~3 that truly fit.** Applying everywhere exhausts the
applicant; fit (geography, theme, stage, eligibility) is the biggest success lever.

## Part D — Criteria mapping (do this before submitting)

This is the mandatory pre-submission pass. Reviewers typically score section by section against stated
criteria; make each one easy to find and award points for.

Build a table from the call's evaluation criteria (or required questions if no rubric is published) and
check the draft against each:

| Criterion / question (from the call) | Weight | Where the draft addresses it | Covered? | Gap / fix |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| e.g. Community leadership | 25% | Para 2 of "Approach"; team section | ✅ | Strengthen the youth-led framing up front |
| e.g. Innovation | 20% | "Approach" para 3 | ⚠️ | Name what's different from prior approaches |
| e.g. Sustainability | 15% | Currently missing | ❌ | Add earned-income + in-kind continuation plan |
| e.g. Budget realism | 15% | Budget table + justification | ✅ | Confirm coordination cost is allowable |
| e.g. Measurable impact | 25% | Objectives + MEL | ⚠️ | Make 2 objectives SMART with dates |

Then verify the mechanics:
- **Word/character/page limits** per section (state the count where limits are tight).
- **Required attachments and format** (PDF/Word, templates, letters of support, registration docs).
- **Eligibility** clearly met, or the access path (fiscal host) named.
- **Names** of funder, programme, project, and people spelled correctly.
- **No copied funder phrases**; priorities reflected in the applicant's own words.
- **No fabricated** numbers, partners, or quotes; placeholders flagged to the user.

Report unmet criteria, over-limit sections, and unconfirmed facts to the user rather than hiding them.
A draft that scores itself honestly against the rubric is far more competitive than one that reads well
but misses a weighted criterion.
